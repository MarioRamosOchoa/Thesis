ACT

The ACT is a standardized test used by college to access your college readiness. There are three main chunks: English, Math, Reading, and Science. In addition, there is an optional Writing component added. You do not need to take this, however, if you feel confident in your writing abilities, it may help your score.

English Section:

English: You will have 45 minutes to complete 75 questions. There will be questions on usage and mechanics, such as correct use of commas and apostrophes, as well as identifying run-on sentences. Additionally there will be questions on sentence structure, grammar, and organization.

Math Section:

Math: This section is 60 minutes long with 60 questions. These questions can range from pre-algebra, algebra, to geometry, and trigonometry. You will be able to use a graphing or scientific calculator in this section, so that will hopefully ease your mind a bit.

Reading Section:

Reading: Time is shortened on this one, as you will have 35 minutes to answer 40 questions. There will be four big sections: Three of them are different passages and the last section has two shorter prose passages. The Reading section is meant to test your ability to quickly identify the main ideas of a passage and the structure the author(s) used. You will likely encounter questions that ask you to find the meaning of a part of the text.

Science Section:

You'll have 35 minutes for this 40 question section. There are seven sections, each with 5-7 questions each. There are three distinct formats that each section could be: Conflicting Viewpoints, Data Representation, and Research Summary.

Writing Section:

You will only complete this section if you want. It will not affect any of the scores you get for the previous section. Instead, you will get a separate writing score. This section is done at the very end of the ACT. You will have 40 minutes to write a well constructed essay on a given prompt. How will your essay be graded? Two trained readers, usually high school teachers that volunteer to do so, will read your paper and give it a score from 1 to 6. The higher the score, the better you did. The readers will be looking for how well you did in each of the following sections: Ideas and Analysis, Development and Support, Organization, Language Use and Conventions. Once they judge each section, the scores are added up. If reader 1 says that your essay deserves a 3 on Organization but reader 2 says you deserve a 6, then a third reader comes in and analyzes your paper. The third reader makes the final decision.

Its Okay to Be Nervous!

This all sounds super scary. But the fear goes away once you're in the middle of doing the exam. Focus on the test and answering the questions as best as possible. If you don't know the answer to a particular question and you're taking too much time on it, then just guess and move on. If you have time at the end, come back to it. But if you use up a ton of time on each question, you won't get all the way through. Its better to guess on those questions and get through the entire test, rather than answer 1/4 of the questions correctly and not complete the test.

Learn more

The Scholastic Aptitude Test, or SAT, is one of the most popular standardized tests used for college admission. The non-profit company, College Board owns the test and you will have to register via their website. The test is supposed to test your college readiness. The test is supposed to reflect the content you have learned in high school.

Final Day to Register for the SAT is

3rd

May

Is it required?

It depends. Most schools will require you to send in your scores from either the SAT or ACT. However, more and more schools are trying out a "test-optional" method, in which you aren't required to send in scores. Each school is different so you will have to check each one individually.

How Long is It?

The SAT will take 3 hours to finish. There is an optional essay component that will take 50 minutes to complete as well. It costs $45 for the test without the essay portion and $57 with the essay portion, therefore it is $12 for the essay portion by itself.

Whats Going to Be On The Test?

You will go through 4 sections: Math with calculator, math without calculator, reading, and writing.

How is it Scored?

The lowest possible score you can get is 400, while a perfect score is 1600. Points are distributed over two sections: Math and Reading & Writing. Each of these two sections is worth 800 points.

What's it Going to Look Like from Start to Finish?

On test day you will bring all the required items to take the test. There will likely be multiple lines. They often are organized alphabetically by last name, so you might find a line for A-L, another for M-R, and S-Z, or some variation of this. Fine your correct line and make your way through. They will check that all of the information you bring is correct. If it is, you will admitted into the test. If it is not, they will pull you aside and likely tell you that you cannot take the test today. In that case you will have to reschedule for another time.


Go to your testing room that the administrators provide. Sit in a seat that looks comfortable. Once everyone in your test group is in the room and seated, the test administrator will read aloud the instructions. Listened up because that information is important. They will then ask if there are any questions. Finally, you will be begin your test. At the end of the exam the test assistant will collect all packets and you are free to go. Helpful Tips From Experience

Make sure the name on your page matches your ID. When filling out the form for the SAT, you will need to input your full name. This includes middle names and multiple last names, if those apply to you. Then you will have to print out a sheet with all of your information and take it into the test center. Once there, the assistants there will ask for a form of identification, here is list of acceptable forms of ID. Make sure the name on your ID and the name on your paper match perfectly. If you go by your middle name and thus put that on the SAT paper, but your ID shows only first name, they will not let you in. They are very strict about this so be careful.


Bring a bag of snacks! You will definitely get hungry during the test and hunger will make you perform worse overall. Instead of thinking about how to solve the math problem you're currently on, you might be thinking of how you would love to be eating a huge stack of pancakes right about now. Plus it makes your stomach growl, which will take even more of the attention off of your test. I'd recommend Cliff bars, cereal bars, fruit, and a drink, like water or juice. These are easy and quick to eat, while still making you feel full.


Show up early. There is always a line to take these sorts of test. The quicker you go through the line, the better. This way you will be able to get in sooner and pick out a seat that works best for you in the testing room. If the seat you first sit in is squeaky, get up and find another, no one likes a squeaky chair. If some of your friends are also taking the test with you, you can sit next to some of them, which can ease your stress a bit. Additionally, by being in the room for a longer period of time, you will be more accustomed to the test setting. The tests can be scary and jarring. If you show up late and rush into the room only to start taking the test soon after can be stressful.


If you don't know the answer, guess. My AP Lit teacher in high school taught us helpful tips for the SAT and ACT. Read the question carefully. If you don't know the answer, first try eliminating the answers that you know are wrong. Once you eliminated all those, then guess. Statistically, I there are 4 possible answers, you have increased your odds by 25% with every answer you eliminated. So, if you eliminated 2 responses as possible answers, that leaves 2 left. If you purely guess from this, statistically you have a 50% chance of getting it right! That is better than 25% you started with originally.


So why would you guess when you can take your time to find the right answer? Well, in these sorts of test, as well as the AP tests, time is very limited. Often times you will run out of time before you can answer all of the questions. So when you run into questions where you have absolutely no idea what the right answer is, instead of taking 5 minutes to carefully pick out an answer, just eliminate options and guess. That will save you a lot of time. Now, if you get through the entire section and still have time at the end, go back to these questions that you guessed on and see if you can answer them now. It is often the case that you encounter a question later on in the packet that jogs your mind and reminds you of how to do a previous one.
