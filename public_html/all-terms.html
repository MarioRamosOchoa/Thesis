ACT

A standardized test used by college to access your college readiness. There are three main chunks: English, Math, Reading, and Science. In addition, there is an optional Writing component added. You do not need to take this, however, if you feel confident in your writing abilities, it may help your score. Learn more in ACT & SAT section!

Admissions Office

The department at a college that receives college applications and makes decisions about which students will be accepted into the university. Admissions representatives attend college fairs, visit high schools, give information session and tours and are a resource for students who are interested in applying.

Advisor

A staff or faculty member at a college that helps students choose classes and a major and connect with other academic support services.

Associate Degree

An undergraduate academic credential that can usually be completed in two years at a community college.

Award Letter

An offer from a college or career school that explains the type and amount of financial aid a school is willing to provide.

Bachelor's Degree

An undergraduate academic credential that is generally obtained in four years at a college or university.

College Credit:

In high school, you can earn college credit via AP and IB classes. They are more rigorous and will challenge you at a college level. Once you take the test you will earn a score between 1 and 4. Each school will individually decide whether or not you will earn college credit based on your score. Most of the time, either a 3 or 4 will earn you credit.

Deferred Admission:

This happens when a college allows you to pause your enrolment in that college after you have been accepted.

Direct Subsidized Loan

A loan based on financial need for which the federal government generally pays the interest that accumulates while the borrower is in an in-school, grace, or deferment status, and during certain periods of repayment under certain income-driven repayment plans.

Direct Unsubsidized Loan

A loan for which the borrower is fully responsible for paying the interest regardless of the loan status. Interest accrues from the date of disbursement and continues throughout the life of the loan.

Early Action:

Basically you apply to a college before the deadline. Colleges will send you their decision a lot sooner than regular submissions. You DO NOT have to enroll in that college.

Early Decision:

Similar to early action, you will submit your application sooner and get a result sooner. However, you are basically saying to the college that you agree to enroll in that college if accepted to the school.

Expected Family Contribution (EFC)

A number that is used to determine your eligibility for federal student financial aid. This number results from the financial information you provide in your FAFSA form and does not change based on the school you attend.

Financial Aid:

Money given to you by the school. This can be in the form of scholarships, loans, grants, work-study, and more. Talk to your financial aid office about how you can get more money for tuition.

Grace Period

For certain types of federal student loans, this is a period of time after you graduate, leave school, or drop below half-time enrollment when you are not required to make payments. You are responsible for paying the interest that accrues on unsubsidized loans during the grace period. If the interest is unpaid, it will be added to the principal balance of the loan (capitalized) when the repayment period begins.

Grant

Financial aid, often based on financial need, which does not need to be repaid.

In-State Tuition

Residents pay lower tuition at public universities and community college than residents of other state because taxpayers help support these institutions. The opposite of this is out of state tuition.

Interest (Loans)

A loan expense charged for the use of borrowed money, paid by a borrower to a lender. Interest is calculated as a percentage, or rate, of the unpaid principal amount of the loan.

Loans

Need-based financial aid that must be paid back.

Loan Repayment Plan

The schedule of how much and how often students pay back their loans. This can be changed at any time for no charge.

Major

Specialized field of study or subject are that a student chooses to study at college.

Master's Degree

An academic credential in a specific field of study that can usually be completed in about two years after getting a bachelor’s degree.

Net Price

An estimate of the actual cost that a student and their family need to pay in a given year to cover education expenses for the student to attend a particular school. Net price is determined by taking the institution’s cost of attendance and then subtracting any gift aid for which the student may receive.

Oregon Opportunity Grant

Oregon's largest state-funded need-based grant program for students planning to go to college. For more information visit their website.

Oregon Promise

A grant program that covers most of the costs for eligible students who plan to attend one of Oregon’s 17 public community colleges.

ORSAA

Oregon Student Aid Application; the form that eligible undocumented and DACA-mented students complete in order to access state financial aid including the Oregon Opportunity Grant, the Oregon Promise grant and some OSAC scholarships. Opens October 1.

OSAC

Office of Student Access and Completion; the agency in Oregon that processes applications for many different scholarships as well as the Oregon Promise program.

Out-of-State Tuition

Usually higher tuition costs than in-state tuition because you state's taxpayers are not providing funding for you to attend.

Pell Grant

Money for college from the federal government that does not need to be paid back; given to undergraduate students with financial need. For more information, go to the FAFSA guide we wrote up or by going to their website.

Perkins Loan

A federal student loan, made by the recipient's school, for undergraduate and graduate students who demonstrate financial need. Read more about it here.

Private Loan

A nonfederal loan made by a lender such as a bank, credit union, state agency, or school. These are alternatives in case your financial aid does not cover enough of your tuition. However, use them sparringly and not at all if possible. They will likely have higher interests than federal loans. Speak to your financial aid office and push for more financial aid because of your financial circumstances.

Retention Rate

The percentage of first-time students seeking bachelor’s degrees who return to the institution to continue their studies the following fall. This is a good measure as to how many students will not return to school.

Room and Board

An allowance for the cost of housing and food while attending college or career school. Some summer jobs, internships, and other programs will offer this to students. It is always nice to have if you are going to be living far away from home.

SAT

A college admission test usually taken during junior or senior year that measures students’ skills. Learn more in our ACT & SAT page.

Undergraduate

Student seeking an associate’s or bachelor’s degree.

Work Study

A federal student aid program that provides part-time employment while you are enrolled in school to help pay your education expenses.
